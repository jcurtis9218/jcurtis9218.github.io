  • Assignment 1

    • The Schwartz
    • WGSL File
    • Video
    • I wanted to explore the stateless nature of shader programming, and I figured the best way for me to understand it was to see if I could recreate something that had the illusion of state. I thought to myself, "what's something that is visually simple, but has a clear progression from one moment to the next?" and my mind immediately jumped to loading bars. Of course, just a rectangle traveling across the screen isn't very interesting. So I followed the common wisdom of how to make something interesting: do it, but like way more. I wanted a multi-stage animation, with both vertical and horizontal bars of different colors, and I especially knew I wanted to get a sort of "woven" look. That's what the first half of the animation turned out to be.

      For the second half of the animation, I wanted to move beyond sliding some rectangles across the screen. The first thing I thought of was having the bars change their width over time, and possibly be more complex than just a solid color. That's when I had the idea of using the bar not as an object to be displayed, but as a mask over another function. This realization allowed me to create a wave that progresses across the screen in much the same way as the earlier bars did. I wanted to create the effect of these waves, which in my mind started to represent a seismograph of sorts, freezing and collapsing. I wanted to keep my animation's timeline scalable, so I couldn't just save a time value and redraw the wave with a constant offset. Instead, I realized that if I set each pixel to whatever it was in the previous frame, that would generate the "freezing" effect I wanted. Afterwards, to create the "collapse" effect, I used a similar technique to set each pixel to the average color of its neighbors, effectively blurring the image. The blur reminded me of a video I watched a while ago regarding smoke simulations, and while I wasn't going to implement anything quite that complicated, I wanted to at least have the mouse leave a colorful trail that disperses over time. With the blur already implemented, all I had to do was set the pixels within a certain distance from the mouse to a set color.
    • Feedback included the following observations:
      • "First like 10 seconds looks like TV static"
      • "Trippy sin wave usage"
      • "I like the mouse tracking stuff, its very pretty
      • "It seems kinda like old school TV"

  • Assignment 2

    • Voronoi Video Warper

    • When I first read the assignment description, I knew I wanted to do something with Voronoi noise. I've used it a decent amount in Blender, and had recently happened across a YouTube video in my recommendations talking about how it worked. Given the requirement to use live video, I came up with the idea of a sort of "broken mirror" effect, using the shard-like nature of Voronoi cells. I figured I could provide the user with the controls for the speed of the animation and what gets displayed in order to satisfy the interaction requirement.
      A lot of the challenge of this project came from creating a three-dimensional grid system to navigate in a two-dimensional space, since I wanted the Voronoi cells to animate smoothly over time. I had a fair bit of difficulty with the disconnect between the CPU and GPU, but eventually I got it to work. One aspect that was particularly challenging was debugging without access to any sort of debugger or console, due to the parallel nature of fragment shaders. It's awfully difficult to figure out what's making the points not see each other if I can't even see the points. Once I got the noise itself working, I had to figure out how to warp the image as it moved. I did this by first noting which point was nearest to the pixel, which it already had to know to make the Voronoi noise in the first place, and figuring out the distance between that point and the pixel itself on both the X and Y axes. I then multiply that vector by (1 - the linear distance to the nearest point), which makes the video get more distorted the closer it is to the edge of the Voronoi cell.

    • The first response I got was that the project was "really weird." It was compared to a funhouse mirror, in that it gets more confusing the longer you look at it. There was confusion about why one would want to draw the grid, which is fair, considering that was mostly a debug tool that I left in to make explaining the concept of Voronoi noise easier. One piece of feedback I'm especially proud of was that it was "pretty fun to watch the shattered mirror effect," especially given that the person providing this feedback had no idea that I had used the exact same phrase in my design intent. This person preferred the animation not moving, because it "made [their] eyes look really large." I imagine that when you don't know what's happening behind the scenes, the shifting motion of the Z-axis travel along the Voronoi grid could be rather headache-inducing.

  • Assignment 3

    • Reaction Diffusion

    • I was intrigued by the idea of biasing the direction of the chemical diffusion. I wanted to see what would happen if the diffusion pattern changed in the middle of the simulation, so I created an interface to bias the direction in real-time. It works by altering the coefficients of the Laplace operation, changing the influence the surrounding pixels have on the current pixel. I also added a pausing feature, which justs sets A'=A and B'=B, so the simulation can be adjusted without the intermediary values altering the result. I thought it would be interesting to look at not only the current values of the pixels, but how much they changed since the previous frame, so I added a "difference" color mode. In this mode, the red channel contains how much the chemical A value changed, and the green channel contains how much the chemical B value changed. This view proves especially interesting when altering the diffusion bias direction.

  • Assignment 4

    • Fluid Simulation

    • The first thing that came to mind in regard to particle simulation was a video I watched a while ago by a YouTuber I follow, Sebastian Lague. He spoke at length about his experience creating and refining a fluid simulation, and I thought to follow his example. In that manner, I developed a particle simulation wherein particles act as samples in a density field, and attempt to maintain a consistent density through pressure forces exerted on them by their neighbors. I was primarily interested in liquid simulation, rather than something gaseous, so I implemented gravity as well. I don't think I quite got the density field calculations correct, since the simulation is rather chaotic, but the general behavior is decently realistic. I wanted to be able to play with the simulation a bit more directly, so I implemented a feature where the mouse can be used to "grab" a section of particles, move them, or throw them across the screen. I also provided controls for altering the target density and pressure scale, though I have tuned the defaults for those parameters to reliable values. The pressure scale in particular is very sensitive. The only "aesthetic" decision I can claim to have made is to make the particles appear blue in color, to vaguely resemble water.
